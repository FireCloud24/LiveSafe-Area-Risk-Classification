About the Data

How LiveSafe works, where the data comes from, and what the results actually mean.

What LiveSafe does

LiveSafe is an area risk classification system — it takes raw crime and weather data and turns it into plain-language labels that describe each local authority area across four dimensions. It's designed so a first-time homebuyer, a property investor, or a council planner can all get useful information from the same system.

Rather than giving each area a single number (like a score out of 100), LiveSafe describes areas across four independent dimensions:

  • Crime Intensity — how much crime is recorded relative to other areas
  • Crime Pattern — whether crime is mostly violent, property-related, or mixed
  • Crime Trend — whether crime is improving, worsening, or fluctuating through 2024
  • Weather Exposure — how much rainfall, cold, and seasonal variation the area experiences

These four labels combine into an overall profile — for example, "Volatile Risk Area" means high crime levels with an unpredictable month-to-month pattern. "Stable Low-Risk Area" means consistently low crime with an improving trend.

What LiveSafe is NOT

  • It is not a predictive policing tool — it does not predict where crime will happen or recommend police deployment
  • It is not a safety score — it describes patterns, not guarantees
  • It is not nationally representative — the prototype covers 15 areas only, and all comparisons are relative within that set
  • It should not be the sole basis for any decision — it is a decision-support tool, not a decision-making engine

Who built it and why

LiveSafe was built as a final-year Data Science & AI project by Myo Satt Kyaw. The goal was to demonstrate that transparent, rule-based classification — where every label can be explained — can be more appropriate than opaque machine learning models in high-stakes civilian contexts.

The system covers 15 local authority areas across England and Wales, selected to represent a range of urban, rural, coastal, and mixed geographies: Leeds, Sheffield, Bristol, Coventry, Newcastle, Leicester, Norwich, Exeter, County Durham, Wiltshire, Herefordshire, North Yorkshire, Cornwall, Cardiff, and Swansea.

Where the data comes from

🚔
UK Police API
Street-level crime data for England and Wales, provided by the Home Office via data.police.uk. For each area, the system downloads every recorded crime incident within the local authority boundary polygon for each month of 2024 — giving 669,646 records in total. Each record includes the crime category (e.g. "violent-crime", "burglary") and outcome status.
data.police.uk/api — free, no API key required
🌦
Meteostat
Daily weather observations (temperature, precipitation) from the nearest weather station to each local authority's geographic centre. Aggregated to monthly averages and totals. 5,490 daily records collected across all 15 areas.
meteostat.net — free Python library
🗺
ONS Open Geography Portal
Local Authority District boundary polygons (LAD 2024 boundaries, EPSG:4326 coordinate system). Used to query the Police API for crimes within each area, calculate geographic centroids for weather lookups, and define the 1,527 neighbourhoods via police force boundaries.
geoportal.statistics.gov.uk — free, open licence
📊
ONS Index of Multiple Deprivation 2019
Used for external validation only — not used in building the classification system. The IMD crime domain scores are compared against LiveSafe's crime intensity rankings using Spearman rank correlation to verify the system captures real patterns. Result: ρ = 0.74 (strong positive, p < 0.01).
MHCLG — English Indices of Deprivation 2019
📍
Postcodes.io
Used for the postcode search feature. Translates a UK postcode into latitude/longitude coordinates and local authority code, allowing the system to identify which area a postcode falls in and find the nearest neighbourhood centroid.
postcodes.io — free, no API key required

Why rules, not a black box?

Classification systems used for housing, investment, or public policy need to be explainable. If a resident asks "why is my area labelled high-risk?", the system should be able to give a clear answer — not "the algorithm decided."

LiveSafe uses transparent rule-based classification as its core method, justified by Rudin (2019): interpretable models should be preferred in high-stakes decision domains because they can achieve comparable accuracy while being explainable to non-technical users.

Crime Intensity — how it's calculated

Based on each area's average monthly crime count across 2024. Thresholds are set at the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles of the 15-area sample, so labels are always relative comparisons — "Severe" means in the top 25% of this dataset.

LabelThreshold
Very Low Recorded CrimeBelow 25th percentile
Moderate Crime Level25th–50th percentile
Elevated Crime Level50th–75th percentile
Severe Crime ConcentrationAbove 75th percentile

Crime Trend — how it's calculated

A linear regression slope is fitted through the 12 monthly crime counts. A positive slope means crime increased month-on-month; negative means it fell. Variability (standard deviation of monthly counts) is checked first — a highly volatile area is labelled "Fluctuating" regardless of slope direction, because a trend is meaningless if counts swing wildly each month.

LabelRule
FluctuatingMonth-to-month variability above 75th percentile (checked first)
ImprovingSlope < −0.5 (and not fluctuating)
StableSlope between −0.5 and +0.5
DeterioratingSlope > +0.5 (and not fluctuating)

Overall Profile — how it combines

The four dimension labels are combined through priority-ordered logic — not a weighted formula, which would require value judgements about how to balance crime against weather.

ProfileCondition
Persistent Multi-Factor Risk AreaHigh/severe crime AND adverse weather
Emerging Risk AreaHigh/severe crime AND deteriorating trend
Volatile Risk AreaHigh/severe crime AND fluctuating trend
Crime-Sensitive AreaHigh/severe crime only
Weather-Sensitive AreaAdverse weather AND low/moderate crime
Structurally Exposed AreaAdverse weather AND moderate-high crime
Stable Low-Risk AreaLow/moderate crime AND stable or improving
Mixed-Risk AreaAll other combinations

Known limitations — be aware of these

📉
Recorded crime ≠ actual crime
The Crime Survey for England and Wales consistently finds roughly twice as many incidents as police records capture. Areas with lower recorded crime may have high actual crime if reporting rates are low. LiveSafe only uses what police record.
📐
Relative labels within 15 areas only
"Severe Crime Concentration" means in the top 25% of this 15-area prototype — not of all areas in England and Wales. An area labelled "Very Low" here might rank differently in a national comparison.
🗺
Boundary simplification
The Police API rejects overly complex polygon queries, so some LA boundaries were simplified before querying. This introduces minor geographic measurement error — a small number of crimes near boundaries may be excluded or mis-attributed.
🌦
Single weather station per area
Weather data comes from the nearest Meteostat station to each LA's geographic centre. For large or coastal areas (Cornwall, North Yorkshire), the nearest station may not represent the whole area's weather.
📅
One year of data
All analysis covers January–December 2024 only. Crime trends are based on 12 monthly data points — a short window that makes trend detection sensitive to individual unusual months. Long-term patterns may differ.
🔮
Forecasts have wide uncertainty ranges
The linear regression forecasts show 95% confidence intervals that are deliberately wide. This is honest — forecasting crime from 12 data points carries high uncertainty. Treat projections as indicative direction, not precise predictions.

Ethical design choices

Several design decisions in LiveSafe were made specifically to avoid known harms in area risk classification systems.

  • Not a predictive policing tool. LiveSafe does not predict where crime will occur, does not target individuals, and does not recommend police deployment. Research by Lum and Isaac (2016) shows predictive systems trained on arrest data systematically reproduce racial and socioeconomic biases in historical policing patterns.
  • No single score. A single number like "Bristol = 72" is indefensible when someone asks why. Presenting dimensions separately avoids the value judgements that come with combining different types of risk into one number (Noble et al., 2019).
  • Relative, not absolute labels. All labels are explicitly comparative within the dataset. This prevents the system from permanently stigmatising areas with strong historical labels that may not reflect current reality.
  • Open data only. No proprietary, personal, or sensitive data is used. All sources are publicly available and free to access.

Appropriate uses

  • Comparing areas when choosing where to live, rent, or invest
  • Identifying areas for further research or investigation by planners
  • Educational exploration of crime data patterns
  • As one input among many in a property or investment analysis

Inappropriate uses

  • As the sole basis for refusing a tenancy, mortgage, or loan application
  • For targeting individuals or communities based on area labels
  • For police resource deployment or operational decisions
  • As a substitute for current, verified local information
